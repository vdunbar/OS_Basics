Countering Systemic Bias

Historically, our society has held the white, heterosexual, male, middle-class or higher, able-bodied adult up as the “norm” and built its institutions to favour him: universities, employment law, and practices for designing medical research, to name a few. One term for this type of favouritism is systemic bias.

In fact, as recently as the end of the Second World War, some scientists helped to promote "scientific racism", encoding these norms through taxonomies and classification systems that grouped humankind into different races. Another discipline that we would now call "pseudoscience", phrenology, attempted to correlate intelligence and personality to observable features of the human skull.

The listed characteristics of privilege above mean that in differing from any of these - whether that be our ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, social class, or ability - we lack that same level of privilege. Equitable practices try to shift this imbalance.

Indigenous Representation in Science

In 2016, 6% of Kelowna’s population identified as Aboriginal, with this term including a number of more specific Indigenous identities, such as Metis, Inuk, and First Nations. If there were no bias in the school and post-secondary educational systems, and assuming that Indigenous people are just as capable as non-Indigenous people at doing science, we would expect to see a comparable percentage of Indigenous people studying and teaching science in colleges and universities. But we don’t.
